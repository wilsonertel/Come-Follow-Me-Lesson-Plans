1

Historical Summary of the Period

This story unfolds about 70 years after the temple was finished — roughly 445 BC, deep in the reign of the Persian emperor Artaxerxes I. Jerusalem had its rebuilt temple, but the city itself still lay open and exposed: its defensive walls broken down and its gates burned ever since Babylon destroyed it in 586 BC. In the ancient world, a city without walls was no real city at all — it was defenseless against raiders, commercially crippled, and a public disgrace. For the returned covenant people, broken walls meant a broken, vulnerable community.

Nehemiah was a Jew who had risen to a position of remarkable trust in the Persian court: cupbearer to the king. He tasted the royal wine to guard against poison, and he had the king's ear daily. When his brother Hanani arrives from Judah with the news that Jerusalem's wall is still broken and its gates burned, Nehemiah weeps, fasts, and prays for days — then asks the king to send him as governor of Judah to rebuild.

The opposition came from three regional officials who did not want a strong, independent Judah on their borders: Sanballat the Horonite, the governor of Samaria; Tobiah the Ammonite; and Geshem the Arabian. They mocked the builders, spread rumors that Nehemiah was plotting rebellion, schemed to attack the workers, and repeatedly tried to lure Nehemiah into a "meeting" where they could harm him.

Concrete anchor · The Elephantine Papyri

On an island in the Nile in southern Egypt, a Jewish military colony left behind about 175 Aramaic documents, preserved for millennia by the dry desert air. One letter — dated about 407 BC, within living memory of Nehemiah's own century — names "Sanballat, governor of Samaria" and his sons Delaiah and Shelemiah. That is a real, dated, non-biblical document naming Nehemiah's chief opponent. (A group of seal impressions later found at Wadi Daliyeh points to the same family.) Nehemiah's enemies were not literary villains; they were historical figures.

Against all of it, the wall was completed in an astonishing 52 days (Nehemiah 6:15) — so fast that even the enemies had to admit "this work was wrought of our God."

Chapter Summary

1
Nehemiah 2:1–8

Sadness before the king

Nehemiah's grief shows on his face. When the king asks why, Nehemiah shoots up a silent prayer "to the God of heaven" in the moment — then boldly asks to be sent to rebuild. The king grants it, with letters of safe passage and timber. Nehemiah credits "the good hand of my God upon me."

2
Nehemiah 2:9–16

The night inspection

Arriving in Jerusalem, Nehemiah tells no one his plan at first. He rides out by night to survey the broken walls and burned gates for himself — counting the cost and making a real plan before asking anyone to commit.

3
Nehemiah 2:17–20

"Let us rise up and build"

He rallies the people; they answer, "Let us rise up and build," and "strengthened their hands for this good work." Sanballat, Tobiah, and Geshem laugh them to scorn and accuse them of rebelling against the king. Nehemiah answers that the God of heaven will prosper them.

4
Nehemiah 4

Building under threat

As the wall reaches half its height, the enemies conspire to attack. Nehemiah posts a round-the-clock guard and arms the workers so that "every one with one of his hands wrought in the work, and with the other hand held a weapon." They labor from first light "till the stars appeared," trusting that "our God shall fight for us."

5
Nehemiah 5

Justice inside the walls

A second front opens — not from enemies, but within. The poor are being crushed by wealthy fellow Jews through crippling debt and even mortgaged children. Nehemiah rebukes the nobles, makes them return what they've taken, and refuses even his own governor's food allowance. He leads by sacrifice, not privilege.

6
Nehemiah 6

Refusing to come down

Sanballat invites Nehemiah four times to "meet" in the plain of Ono; four times Nehemiah answers, "I am doing a great work, so that I cannot come down." Threats and a hired false prophet fail to make him flee. The wall is finished in 52 days, and the enemies are cast down, "for they perceived that this work was wrought of our God."

On the "sword and trowel" (Nehemiah 4:16–18)

This isn't a lesson in violence — it's a picture of faithful vigilance. The builders worked with everything they had and stayed ready, trusting God to fight for them. Diligence and watchfulness go together. Nehemiah's charge sums it up: "Be not ye afraid of them: remember the Lord, which is great and terrible" (Nehemiah 4:14).

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Nehemiah's courage under fire has close cousins throughout the Book of Mormon.

Nehemiah 4:16–18

The builders work with a tool in one hand and a weapon in the other, guarding the work as they build.

Book of Mormon echo

The Nephites fortify and defend their cities while continuing to build and live righteously (Alma 48:7–10; 50:1–6).

Teaches: God's people both work and watch — diligence and defense belong together.
Nehemiah 6:3

Nehemiah refuses to "come down" and abandon the work, no matter how the enemy baits him.

Book of Mormon echo

Abinadi will not take back his message even under threat of death: "I will not recall the words which I have spoken" (Mosiah 17:9–10).

Teaches: Stay on the errand God gave you; don't let anyone talk or scare you off it.
Nehemiah 2:17–18

Nehemiah rallies a discouraged people: "Let us rise up and build."

Book of Mormon echo

Captain Moroni raises the title of liberty and rallies the people to defend their families, freedom, and faith (Alma 46:12–13, 20).

Teaches: A righteous leader can inspire ordinary people to rise to a great cause.
Nehemiah 5:14–18

Nehemiah refuses his governor's allowance and defends the poor from their own leaders.

Book of Mormon echo

King Benjamin labors with his own hands so as not to burden his people (Mosiah 2:12–14).

Teaches: True leaders serve and sacrifice rather than take advantage of their position.
Nehemiah 6:16

The enemies see the finished wall and know the work "was wrought of our God."

Book of Mormon echo

Ammon gives all credit to God: "I know that I am nothing… but the Lord… has all power" (Alma 26:11–12).

Teaches: When the work succeeds against the odds, the glory belongs to God.

Tie to Come, Follow Me

The wall chapters carry the week's central, seminary-marked principle: God has an important work for me to do.

What the outline points to in these chapters

  • "God has an important work for me to do" (Ezra 4–7; Nehemiah 2; 4; 6). The outline offers a compare-your-life chart: God's work for Nehemiah (Nehemiah 2:17–18) · the opposition he faced (Nehemiah 2:19; 4:1–3, 7–8; 6:1–13) · how he responded (Nehemiah 2:20; 4:6, 9; 6:3–15) — with a matching line for the work, opposition, and response in your life.
  • Two phrases to sit with: what it means to have "a mind to work" in the Savior's service (Nehemiah 4:6), and what it means to feel "the good hand of … God upon [you]" as you serve (Nehemiah 2:8, 18).
  • The talk the outline cites: President Dieter F. Uchtdorf, "We Are Doing a Great Work and Cannot Come Down" (May 2009) — especially the last two sections, which apply Nehemiah's line directly to us.
  • Also suggested: the Young Women Theme, the Aaronic Priesthood Quorum Theme, and hymns "As Sisters in Zion" (no. 309) and "Ye Elders of Israel" (no. 319).
  • Scripture Help for this block: "Why was it so important to rebuild Jerusalem's walls?" — answered in the history section above. Bible Dictionary: "Nehemiah."

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Build the outline's three-column chart on the board, then fill it in for a real challenge in their lives — being a good example at school, keeping a standard online. What's the work? What's the opposition? How will they respond like Nehemiah?
  • Unpack "sword and trowel." What does it look like to keep building while resisting pressure? Give modern examples (peer pressure, phone distractions) and have them name their own "one hand builds, one hand guards."
  • Memorize Nehemiah 6:3 together as a one-line comeback to distraction. Read or watch the last two sections of President Uchtdorf's talk and ask what "great work" they will not come down from.
  • Notice that Nehemiah prayed a split-second prayer before answering the king (Nehemiah 2:4). Practice "arrow prayers" — quick, silent prayers in the moment before a hard conversation or decision.
