Historical Summary of the Period

This week covers roughly a century and a half of the post-exilic period — the return from Babylon and the rebuilding of Jewish life. About 586 BC, Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon had razed Jerusalem, burned Solomon's temple, and carried Judah into a 70-year captivity, just as Jeremiah foretold.

Then the world changed hands. In 539 BC, Cyrus the Great of Persia conquered Babylon, and his empire ran on a policy of sending exiles home and restoring their temples. In 538 BC he decreed that the Jews could return to rebuild the house of the Lord — funding it and returning the sacred vessels. The first group of about 50,000, led by Zerubbabel (of David's royal line) and Jeshua the high priest, laid the temple's foundation about 536 BC. Opposition stalled the work for years until the prophets Haggai and Zechariah stirred the people in 520 BC and emperor Darius I confirmed Cyrus's decree; the temple was finished in 516/515 BC.

Decades later, two more leaders arrive under Artaxerxes I: the scribe Ezra about 458 BC, sent to teach the law, and Nehemiah — the king's own cupbearer — about 445 BC, who returned as governor and rebuilt Jerusalem's broken walls in a stunning 52 days. Ezra and Nehemiah were contemporaries; the book's order groups events by theme rather than strict chronology.

Two anchors you can hold in your hand

The Cyrus Cylinder (British Museum, unearthed at Babylon in 1879) records Cyrus's real policy of returning deported peoples and restoring their sanctuaries — echoing Ezra 1, though it credits the god Marduk where Ezra credits Jehovah. And the Elephantine papyri from Egypt (c. 407 BC) name "Sanballat, governor of Samaria" — Nehemiah's chief opponent — proving he was a genuine historical figure. Scripture's account of the return is grounded in the documented history of the Persian Empire.

Passage Summary

The week's chapters fall into three "rebuildings." Here's the whole story in six beats.

Rebuilding the Temple · Ezra 1; 3–6
1
Ezra 1; 3

The decree and the foundation

The Lord "stirs up" Cyrus, who frees the Jews and restores the temple vessels. Back in Jerusalem, the returnees rebuild the altar first and resume worship, then lay the temple's foundation with singing — while the old weep for Solomon's lost temple and the young shout for joy, the two sounds blending into one.

2
Ezra 4–6

Opposition — then completion

Adversaries "weaken the hands" of the builders and halt the work by letters to the king. Years later Haggai and Zechariah stir the people; Zerubbabel rises and builds; Darius finds and confirms Cyrus's decree. The temple is finished in 516 BC and dedicated with a joyful Passover.

Rebuilding the Wall · Nehemiah 2; 4–6
3
Nehemiah 2

The call and the start

Nehemiah, cupbearer in Susa, hears the wall is broken and the gates burned. He prays, then boldly asks the king to send him. He inspects the ruins by night, then rallies the people: "Let us rise up and build" — while Sanballat, Tobiah, and Geshem mock and accuse.

4
Nehemiah 4–6

Building under fire — and finishing

As enemies plot attacks, the workers build with a tool in one hand and a weapon in the other. Nehemiah also defends the poor from their own leaders and refuses his governor's allowance. Lured four times to "come down," he answers, "I am doing a great work, so that I cannot come down." The wall is finished in 52 days.

Rebuilding the People · Ezra 7; Nehemiah 8
5
Ezra 7

The scribe and the commission

Ezra, "a ready scribe in the law of Moses," "prepared his heart to seek the law of the Lord, and to do it, and to teach" (7:10). Artaxerxes sends him with funds and authority; Ezra credits "the good hand of his God upon him."

6
Nehemiah 8

The word read — and lived

The whole people gather at the Water Gate and ask for the book. Ezra reads it from a wooden platform while Levites "give the sense" so all can understand. The people weep at how far they'd drifted, then are lifted — "the joy of the Lord is your strength" — and go keep the Feast of Tabernacles because they finally understood.

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Six of the strongest parallels across all three rebuildings.

Ezra 1 · Temple

The Lord stirs up Cyrus, a king outside the covenant, to free and fund His people.

Book of Mormon echo

Nephi foresees the Lord using the Gentiles to carry and restore scattered Israel (1 Nephi 22:7–8).

Teaches: God works through people outside His Church to accomplish His purposes.
Ezra 3 · Temple

At the foundation, weeping and joy blend into one overwhelming sound.

Book of Mormon echo

Benjamin's people fall down, overcome with joy and a sense of their nothingness before God (Mosiah 4:1–3).

Teaches: Nearness to the Lord stirs both sorrow for the past and joy in redemption.
Nehemiah 4 · Wall

Builders work with a tool in one hand and a weapon in the other.

Book of Mormon echo

The Nephites build and defend their cities at once (Alma 48:7–10; 50:1–6).

Teaches: God's people both work and watch — diligence and defense together.
Nehemiah 6 · Wall

Nehemiah will not "come down" from the work, whatever the bait.

Book of Mormon echo

Abinadi will not recall his words even facing death (Mosiah 17:9–10).

Teaches: Stay on the errand God gave you; don't be talked or scared off it.
Nehemiah 8 · People

The people gather to hear the word read from a wooden platform, then covenant.

Book of Mormon echo

King Benjamin proclaims the word from a tower; the people covenant to obey (Mosiah 2:7–8; 5:2–5).

Teaches: God's people gather to hear His word and bind themselves to live it.
Ezra 7 · People

Ezra "prepared his heart to seek… do… and teach" the law.

Book of Mormon echo

The sons of Mosiah "searched the scriptures diligently" and taught with power (Alma 17:2–3).

Teaches: Real gospel learning moves from seeking, to living, to teaching.

Tie to Come, Follow Me

The official outline for this week draws four principles from these chapters. All four are below, with the questions and helps the manual cites.

The week's four principles

  • 1 · "The Lord inspires people to accomplish His purposes" (Ezra 1). How do you see the Lord working through people around you — including those who are not members of His Church? See Isaiah 44:24–28, where Cyrus is named ~150 years early.
  • 2 · "The house of the Lord is a place of joy" (Ezra 3:8–13; 6:16–22). Set the burned temple (2 Kings 25; Psalm 137) against the later celebration. Why is building a temple a reason to celebrate?
  • 3 · "God has an important work for me to do" (Ezra 4–7; Nehemiah 2; 4; 6). The seminary-marked principle — the Lord's work rarely goes unopposed. Study President Dieter F. Uchtdorf's "We Are Doing a Great Work and Cannot Come Down" (May 2009). What does it mean to have "a mind to work" (Nehemiah 4:6) and to feel "the good hand of … God upon [you]" (Nehemiah 2:8, 18; Ezra 7:6, 9, 27–28)?
  • 4 · "I am blessed when I study the scriptures" (Nehemiah 8). A people long cut off from the word hear it read and explained. See also Teachings of Presidents: Ezra Taft Benson, ch. 8, "The Power of the Word" (pp. 115–24).

Scripture Helps & Bible Dictionary for the week

Three questions the outline raises: Why did the Jews reject the Samaritans' offer to help rebuild the temple? How was Zerubbabel's temple different from Solomon's? Why was it so important to rebuild Jerusalem's walls? And the Bible Dictionary entries "Ezra" and "Nehemiah" give helpful overviews of both books and men.

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Anchor the whole lesson on the chart above. Build it together on the board, then have each youth privately fill in the "Me" row and share if they're willing.
  • Memorize Nehemiah 6:3 as a one-line answer to distraction, and read or watch the last two sections of President Uchtdorf's talk.
  • Show the Cyrus Cylinder and note Isaiah named Cyrus ~150 years early — discuss how fulfilled prophecy builds faith.
  • Recreate the Water Gate: read a few verses "distinctly," then have the class "give the sense" in their own words, using footnotes and the Gospel Library study aids.
  • Close on "the joy of the Lord is your strength": invite each youth to name one thing from this week's reading to actually do before next Sunday.
