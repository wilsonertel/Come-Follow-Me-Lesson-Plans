Historical Summary of the Period

These chapters open the post-exilic period of Israel's history — the return from Babylon. A century earlier, Nebuchadnezzar's armies had razed Jerusalem, burned Solomon's temple to the ground (about 586 BC), and carried Judah into captivity. The people spent roughly 70 years in Babylon, just as Jeremiah had prophesied (Jeremiah 29:10). The temple was gone, the covenants were half-forgotten — but the promise of a return still stood.

In 539 BC, Cyrus the Great of Persia conquered Babylon, which fell almost without a fight. Cyrus ran his vast empire — stretching from the Aegean to the Indus — on a policy unusual for the ancient world: rather than deporting and crushing conquered peoples, he sent exiles home and helped restore their temples. In 538 BC he issued a decree that the Jews could return to Jerusalem and rebuild the house of the Lord, even funding the project from the royal treasury and returning the sacred gold and silver vessels Nebuchadnezzar had looted.

Geography: the journey home ran roughly 900 miles. The returnees came back to a tiny, impoverished Persian province called Yehud, its capital Jerusalem still in ruins, ringed by suspicious neighbors — chiefly the mixed population of Samaria to the north.

The first group of about 50,000 was led by Zerubbabel, a descendant of King David and heir of the royal line, together with Jeshua the high priest. They laid the temple's foundation about 536 BC, but opposition stalled the work for years. In 520 BC the prophets Haggai and Zechariah stirred the discouraged people to build again; the emperor Darius I confirmed Cyrus's original decree after a search of the royal archives, and the second temple was completed and dedicated in 516/515 BC.

Concrete anchor · The Cyrus Cylinder

In 1879 archaeologists at Babylon unearthed a small barrel-shaped clay cylinder inscribed with 45 lines of Akkadian cuneiform — now in the British Museum. Commissioned by Cyrus himself, it records his policy of returning deported peoples to their homelands and restoring their sanctuaries, strikingly paralleling Ezra 1. (The cylinder credits the Babylonian god Marduk with stirring Cyrus; Ezra credits Jehovah — Ezra 1:1.) It is a tangible, non-biblical witness that the return really happened the way scripture describes.

Chapter Summary

1
Ezra 1

The decree of Cyrus

"The Lord stirred up the spirit of Cyrus." He proclaims that the Jews may go up to Jerusalem to build the house of the Lord, invites their neighbors to help with silver, gold, and goods, and restores about 5,400 temple vessels Nebuchadnezzar had carried off.

2
Ezra 3:1–7

The altar comes first

Gathered "as one man" in Jerusalem, the returnees rebuild the altar and resume daily sacrifices and the Feast of Tabernacles — restoring worship before a single wall or temple stone is raised. Priorities first: they reconnect with God, then build.

3
Ezra 3:8–13

The foundation is laid

With trumpets and singing — "for he is good, for his mercy endureth for ever" — they lay the foundation. The sound is bittersweet: the young shout for joy while the old, who remembered Solomon's temple, weep aloud. The two sounds blend so that no one could tell them apart.

4
Ezra 4

Opposition halts the work

Local adversaries offer to help build; the Jews decline, to keep the covenant work undivided and properly authorized. The adversaries then "weaken the hands" of the people, hire counselors against them, and send accusing letters to the king — stalling the project for years.

5
Ezra 5

The work resumes

The prophets Haggai and Zechariah preach; Zerubbabel and Jeshua "rise up" and build again. When the Persian governor Tattenai questions their authority, they appeal to Cyrus's original decree, and a letter is sent to Darius to verify it.

6
Ezra 6

Darius confirms — and it is finished

The original decree is found in the archives at Ecbatana. Darius not only allows the work but orders it funded from royal revenue. The temple is finished in his sixth year (516 BC) and dedicated with joy; the people keep the Passover, "for the Lord had made them joyful."

For the hard question · Why refuse the Samaritans' help? (Ezra 4:1–3)

This can read like ethnic snobbery, but the issue is covenant integrity, not race. The "people of the land" were the mixed population resettled in the north, whose worship blended Jehovah with the gods of other nations (see 2 Kings 17:24–33). Joining the Lord's temple to a compromised, unauthorized worship would have corrupted it from the start. Zerubbabel keeps the work pure and under proper priesthood authority — the same reason we're careful today about who administers sacred ordinances.

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Every parallel below rests on a shared doctrinal pattern, not just a similar scene.

Ezra 1

The Lord stirs up Cyrus, a king who does not worship Him, to free and fund the covenant people.

Book of Mormon echo

Nephi foresees the Lord using the Gentiles — outsiders to the covenant — to carry, protect, and restore scattered Israel (1 Nephi 22:7–8; 1 Nephi 13:12–19).

Teaches: God works through people outside His Church to accomplish His purposes.
Ezra 3, 6

Far from the old land, the people build a house of the Lord as the center of their restored life.

Book of Mormon echo

In the new world, Nephi builds a temple after the pattern of Solomon's (2 Nephi 5:16).

Teaches: Wherever the covenant people are gathered, they build a temple to worship God.
Ezra 3:1

The returnees gather "as one man" to Jerusalem to restore worship together.

Book of Mormon echo

King Benjamin's people gather as families to the temple to hear the word and enter a covenant (Mosiah 2:1–6).

Teaches: The Lord gathers His covenant people to His house to be taught and to promise obedience.
Ezra 3:11–13

At the foundation, weeping and shouting for joy blend into one overwhelming sound.

Book of Mormon echo

At the temple, Benjamin's people fall to the earth, overcome with joy and a view of their own nothingness before God (Mosiah 4:1–3).

Teaches: Drawing near the Lord's presence stirs deep, mixed feeling — sorrow for the past and joy in redemption.
Ezra 1–2

God brings His people out of captivity in Babylon and home to their land.

Book of Mormon echo

The Lord delivers Alma's people from bondage under Amulon, easing their burdens and leading them to freedom (Mosiah 24:16–22).

Teaches: In His own time, God delivers His covenant people from captivity.

Tie to Come, Follow Me

Of the week's four principles, these temple chapters carry the first two — the Lord's power to move history, and the joy of His house.

What the outline points to in these chapters

  • "The Lord inspires people to accomplish His purposes" (Ezra 1). The outline asks: how do you see the Lord working through people around you, including people who are not members of His Church? See also Isaiah 44:24–28, where Isaiah names Cyrus by name — roughly 150 years before he was born.
  • "The house of the Lord is a place of joy" (Ezra 3:8–13; 6:16–22). Set the sorrow of the burned temple (2 Kings 25:1–10; Psalm 137) against the celebration decades later. The outline asks: Why is the building of a temple a reason to celebrate?
  • Scripture Helps for this block: "How was Zerubbabel's temple different from Solomon's temple?" and "Why did the Jews reject the Samaritans' offer to help rebuild the temple?" — both answered in this handout.
  • Bible Dictionary: "Ezra" gives an overview of the book and the man behind it.

Like Zerubbabel's day, ours is an age of temple building — and youth do the Lord's work there in baptisms for the dead.

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Show a picture of the Cyrus Cylinder. Ask: how could a king who didn't worship God be part of God's plan? Name people they know who aren't members but clearly do good — how might God be working through them?
  • Read Ezra 3:11–13 and describe the two crowds — one weeping, one shouting. Ask each youth what makes the temple a place of joy for them, and invite anyone who's done baptisms for the dead to share.
  • Point out that Isaiah named Cyrus about 150 years early (Isaiah 44:28; 45:1). Discuss how God's foreknowledge — and fulfilled prophecy — can strengthen their own faith.
  • List every obstacle the builders hit (Ezra 4) and how they got past each one. Then have the class name a hard, right thing they're trying to "build" and one way to keep going when it stalls.
