Historical Summary of the Period

When and who. Jehoshaphat, Asa's son, reigned about 873–849 BC (25 years, including a short co-regency with his aging father). He ruled Judah while the infamous Ahab and Jezebel ruled the northern Kingdom of Israel — the very years of Elijah's ministry, Mount Carmel, and Naboth's vineyard. The two kingdoms, enemies for two generations, made peace; Jehoshaphat sealed it by marrying his son Jehoram to Ahab's daughter Athaliah — an alliance that would later nearly destroy the royal line of David.

Israel on the world stage. Assyrian records give us a rare outside glimpse of this exact moment: the Kurkh Monolith of Shalmaneser III lists "Ahab the Israelite" contributing thousands of chariots to a coalition that fought Assyria at Qarqar in 853 BC. Shortly after, Ahab died at Ramoth-gilead — the battle of 2 Chronicles 18, where Jehoshaphat nearly died at his side. The Mesha Stele (the "Moabite Stone," c. 840 BC) records Moab's side of its rebellion against Israel in this era — the same restless Moab that, with Ammon and Mount Seir (Edom), marched on Judah in chapter 20.

The geography of chapter 20. The invaders came around the south end of the Dead Sea and up its western shore to En-gedi ("Hazazon-tamar"), then climbed the desert ascent toward Tekoa, aiming at Jerusalem from the southeast — a route that gave Judah almost no warning. When the threat evaporated, the army gathered in a valley they named Berachah, "blessing," a name the chronicler says still stuck in his own day.

A kingdom built on teaching. What makes Jehoshaphat unusual among kings is chapter 17: in his third year he sent princes, Levites, and priests on a circuit through all the cities of Judah "and had the book of the law of the Lord with them" (17:9). He also appointed honest judges city by city with the charge, "ye judge not for man, but for the Lord" (19:6). The chronicler links these reforms directly to Judah's security: surrounding nations feared to make war.

Anchor: a battle with a paper trail

The battle of Ramoth-gilead (ch. 18) sits within a few years of two datable artifacts — the Kurkh Monolith naming Ahab (853 BC) and the Mesha Stele describing Moab's revolt. Your class is reading about kings whose names appear in their enemies' own stone inscriptions, on display today in the British Museum and the Louvre.

Chapter Summary

1
17:1–19

A kingdom taught, a kingdom safe

Jehoshaphat "sought to the Lord God of his father" and strengthened Judah — but his signature move is sending traveling teachers with the book of the law through every city. "The fear of the Lord fell upon all the kingdoms" around Judah, and no one made war.

2
18:1–11

A dangerous friendship

Allied by marriage to Ahab, Jehoshaphat is feasted in Samaria and pressured to join a war for Ramoth-gilead. To his credit he asks, "Enquire … at the word of the Lord to day." Four hundred court prophets promise victory in unison. Jehoshaphat senses the flattery: "Is there not here a prophet of the Lord besides?"

3
18:12–27

Micaiah stands alone

The messenger begs Micaiah to match the majority. Micaiah answers, "even what my God saith, that will I speak" (18:13). After a sarcastic echo of the court prophets (v. 14), his real prophecy (v. 16): Israel scattered "as sheep that have no shepherd" — Ahab will die. He is slapped, mocked, and jailed on bread and water. He goes anyway.

4
18:28–34

The prophecy no disguise could dodge

Ahab disguises himself and puts Jehoshaphat in the royal robes — making his ally the target. Jehoshaphat cries out and "the Lord helped him." A Syrian archer draws his bow "at a venture" (at random) and the arrow finds the seam in Ahab's armor. Micaiah's word stands.

5
19:1–11

Rebuked, then reforming

The seer Jehu meets the returning king: "Shouldest thou help the ungodly …?" Jehoshaphat accepts the correction and doubles down on goodness — personally traveling among the people to bring them back to the Lord, and appointing judges who fear God, take no bribes, and act "in the fear of the Lord, faithfully, and with a perfect heart."

6
20:1–13

Surrounded — so he gathers everyone to pray

Moab, Ammon, and Mount Seir advance together; they're already at En-gedi. Jehoshaphat "feared, and set himself to seek the Lord, and proclaimed a fast." All Judah gathers at the temple — "their little ones, their wives, and their children" — and the king prays, recalling God's past deliverances and Solomon's temple promise, ending: "we have no might … neither know we what to do: but our eyes are upon thee" (20:12).

7
20:14–19

God's answer through Jahaziel

The Spirit rests on a Levite in the congregation: "Be not afraid … for the battle is not yours, but God's" (20:15). Tomorrow, go down and "stand ye still, and see the salvation of the Lord." The king and people fall down and worship; the Levites stand up and praise "with a loud voice on high."

8
20:20–30

Singers in front of the army

At dawn Jehoshaphat charges the people: "Believe in the Lord your God, so shall ye be established; believe his prophets, so shall ye prosper" (20:20). He appoints singers to march ahead of the army praising "the beauty of holiness." As the singing begins, the Lord sets ambushments; the enemy coalition turns on itself. Judah spends three days gathering spoil, blesses God in the valley of Berachah, and returns to Jerusalem with psalteries, harps, and trumpets. "The realm of Jehoshaphat was quiet: for his God gave him rest."

Teacher's note on 20:35–37

The chapter ends with one more gentle warning: Jehoshaphat later joins a shipping venture with Ahab's wicked son Ahaziah, and the ships are wrecked before they ever sail. Even a great man's recurring weakness — entangling alliances — follows him. God corrects those He loves, to the end.

"We have no might against this great company that cometh against us; neither know we what to do: but our eyes are upon thee."2 Chronicles 20:12

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Every beat of Jehoshaphat's story — the teaching, the lone prophet, the fast, the deliverance — has a Nephite twin.

2 Chr 17:7–9

Jehoshaphat's best defense strategy: teachers circulating through every city with the book of the law.

Alma 31:5

Alma resigns the judgment seat to preach, because the word "had a more powerful effect upon the minds of the people … than the sword."

Teaches: Teaching the word of God protects a people better than armies do.
2 Chr 18:13

Micaiah, alone against 400 flattering prophets: "what my God saith, that will I speak" — then jail.

Mosiah 12–17

Abinadi, alone before King Noah's priests, testifies knowing it may cost his life: "I finish my message; and then it matters not."

Teaches: One person with God's word outweighs any majority — and courage like that changes history (Micaiah's word felled Ahab; Abinadi's converted Alma).
2 Chr 20:3–4

Facing invasion, Jehoshaphat proclaims a fast, and "Judah gathered themselves together, to ask help of the Lord."

Mosiah 27:22–23

The church fasts and prays together for Alma the Younger — and a united fast brings a miracle.

Teaches: Fasting gathers scattered faith into one — communities that fast together see deliverance together.
2 Chr 20:15–17

"The battle is not yours, but God's … stand ye still, and see the salvation of the Lord."

Alma 57:25–27

Helaman's 2,060 young warriors all survive against impossible odds "because of their exceeding faith in that which they had been taught."

Teaches: When God owns the battle, the outcome doesn't depend on our numbers — it depends on our trust.
2 Chr 20:20

"Believe his prophets, so shall ye prosper" — the king's final charge before the miracle.

1 Ne 15:24–25

Those who "hearken unto the word of God, and … hold fast unto it" never perish — Nephi's iron rod is the same promise in a different image.

Teaches: Heeding prophets isn't optional extra credit; it's the grip on the rod that gets us through the mist.
2 Chr 20:21–22

Singers march in front of the army; the victory begins "when they began to sing and to praise."

Alma 26:8, 16

Ammon "glories" and sings praise after God delivers thousands of Lamanites — praise as the natural language of trust and triumph.

Teaches: Praise isn't what we do after God wins; sometimes it's how the winning starts.

  • "I can defend the truth, even when it's unpopular" (2 Chronicles 18). The outline walks through the pressure on Micaiah (vv. 1–12), his courage and integrity (vv. 13–27 — noting v. 14 is sarcasm and v. 16 is his real answer), and the fulfillment (vv. 28–34). Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Role-play Micaiah's dilemma: 400 voices say one thing, God says another. What does that pressure sound like at school or online in 2026? Use the manual's question — what gives you courage?
  • Mark Jehoshaphat's four moves in 20:3–12 (he feared → he sought the Lord → he fasted → he gathered and prayed), then have each youth translate one move into something they can actually do during their next hard week.
  • Comfort-phrase hunt in 20:14–17: find a phrase for someone who's afraid, write it on a card, and decide who could use it this week.
  • Question marks → exclamation points: list three recent teachings from the living prophet; discuss President Nelson's counsel about acting on them instead of debating them.
  • Open with a hymn before the lesson's climax, then reveal 20:21–22: the singers went first, and the victory began when the singing began. Ask how music invites God's power into their week.

Tie to Jesus Christ

"Our eyes are upon thee" is more than a prayer about one battle — it's the gospel's whole posture toward the Savior.

Eyes upon Him: look and live

Jehoshaphat's confession — no might, no plan, only eyes on God (20:12) — is the very act the Savior asks of us. He compared Himself to the brass serpent Moses lifted up: "as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: that whosoever believeth in him should not perish" (John 3:14–15). Alma taught it as the easiest and hardest thing in scripture: "look to God and live" (Alma 37:47). The week's title is an invitation to fix our eyes on Christ.

The battle He fought for us

"The battle is not yours, but God's … stand ye still, and see the salvation of the Lord" (20:15, 17). This is a type of the Atonement: the decisive battle against sin and death was one we had no might to fight, and Jesus fought it alone — "I have trodden the wine-press alone" (D&C 76:107). Judah's job was to trust, show up, and receive a salvation they did not win. So is ours: His grace saves, "after all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23).

He sang on the way to His hardest battle

Judah marched to war behind singers praising God (20:21–22) — and the victory began with the song. On the night of Gethsemane, Jesus and His apostles did the same thing: "when they had sung an hymn, they went out into the mount of Olives" (Matthew 26:30). The Savior Himself chose praise as the doorway into His greatest trial — a pattern He set and Judah's choir foreshadowed.

The King who pleads for His people

Jehoshaphat stood in the temple and pleaded his people's case before God (20:5–12). Christ does this perfectly and forever: He is our "advocate with the Father," pleading our cause — "Father, behold the sufferings and death of him who did no sin" (D&C 45:3–4; 1 John 2:1). Every time a leader intercedes for his people in scripture, we're seeing a preview of Jesus.
