Historical Summary of the Period

What Chronicles is. First and Second Chronicles were originally one Hebrew book, compiled after the Babylonian exile (c. 450–400 BC) for Jews rebuilding a shattered nation. Where Kings tells the story of both kingdoms, Chronicles focuses almost entirely on Judah, the house of David, and the temple — retelling the past to answer one question for a returned remnant: are we still God's covenant people, and what does He require of us? (See Bible Dictionary, "Chronicles.") That's why every king's story is graded on a single scale: did he seek the Lord?

The divided kingdom. After Solomon died (c. 931 BC), the nation split: ten tribes formed the northern Kingdom of Israel; David's line kept the southern Kingdom of Judah with Jerusalem and the temple. This week's four kings span about two centuries of Judah's story: Asa (c. 911–870 BC), his son Jehoshaphat (c. 873–849 BC), then — a century later — Uzziah (c. 792–740 BC), and Hezekiah (c. 715–686 BC), who reigned just after Assyria destroyed the northern kingdom in 722 BC.

These kings left fingerprints. Jehoshaphat's ally Ahab appears in Assyria's Kurkh Monolith (853 BC), and Moab's revolt from that era is told on the Mesha Stele. A reburial plaque — the Uzziah Tablet, now in the Israel Museum — marks the moved bones of "Uzziah, king of Judah." And a clay seal impression reading "Belonging to Hezekiah, [son of] Ahaz, king of Judah" was excavated at the foot of the Temple Mount and announced in 2015. Your class is studying people whose names survive in stone and clay.

The through-line the chronicler wants you to see

Each reign is a controlled experiment in the same law. Asa: decades of trust, then a drift into self-reliance. Jehoshaphat: total dependence — "our eyes are upon thee" — and total deliverance. Uzziah: "marvellously helped, till he was strong," then pride and a fall. Hezekiah: humility, mercy, and joy not seen since Solomon. Four kings, one verdict: "the Lord is with you, while ye be with him" (15:2).

Chapter Summary — Four Movements

1
2 Chr 14–16 · Asa

A strong start, a sad finish

Young Asa purges idolatry, and when Zerah's uncountable Cushite army attacks, his battle plan is a prayer: "we rest on thee" (14:11) — and God routs the invaders. The prophet Azariah states the week's law: "The Lord is with you, while ye be with him" (15:2), and Judah renews its covenant with shouting and joy. But thirty-five years later, blockaded by northern Israel, Asa hires Syria with temple treasure instead of praying, imprisons the seer Hanani who rebukes him, and dies seeking "not to the Lord, but to the physicians" (16:12). Trust, it turns out, must be renewed for a lifetime.

2
2 Chr 17–20 · Jehoshaphat

The chapters the week is named for

Jehoshaphat defends Judah by sending traveling teachers with the book of the law (17:9) and honest judges who "judge … for the Lord" (19:6). Entangled with wicked Ahab, he watches the lone prophet Micaiah defy 400 flatterers — "what my God saith, that will I speak" (18:13) — and be proven right by a random arrow. Then three armies converge, and the king calls a fast and prays before all Judah: "we have no might … neither know we what to do: but our eyes are upon thee" (20:12). God's answer through Jahaziel: "the battle is not yours, but God's" (20:15). Judah marches out behind a choir, the enemy destroys itself, and the valley is renamed Blessing.

3
2 Chr 26 · Uzziah

When he was strong, his heart was lifted up

Crowned at sixteen, mentored by Zechariah, Uzziah proves 26:5: "as long as he sought the Lord, God made him to prosper." Fifty-two years of towers, wells, armies, and invented war engines follow — "he was marvellously helped, till he was strong. But when he was strong, his heart was lifted up to his destruction" (26:15–16). He storms the temple to burn incense — an ordinance God reserved to consecrated priests — rages at the eighty brave priests who withstand him, and is struck with leprosy, living out his days cut off from the very temple he presumed upon.

4
2 Chr 30 · Hezekiah

A Passover for former enemies

Just after the northern kingdom falls to Assyria, Hezekiah sends runners across the old border inviting the shattered remnant to keep Passover in Jerusalem: "turn again unto the Lord … he will not turn away his face from you" (30:9). Some mock; some humble themselves and come. When northern guests eat the feast ritually unprepared, the king prays — "The good Lord pardon every one that prepareth his heart" — and the Lord "healed the people" (30:18–20). Joy runs so high the assembly doubles the feast to fourteen days: the greatest celebration since Solomon.

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Mormon abridged a thousand years of his people's history around the very pattern these four reigns teach.

The whole week's pattern

Sought the Lord → prospered → grew strong → forgot Him → fell. Asa and Uzziah live the full cycle; Jehoshaphat and Hezekiah break it through humility.

Helaman 12:1–2

"At the very time when he doth prosper his people … then is the time that they do harden their hearts, and do forget the Lord their God."

Teaches: The pride cycle isn't ancient trivia — it's the recurring physics of the human heart, on both continents and in our own lives.
Asa · 2 Chr 16

The king who once prayed down an army ends up hiring Syria and trusting physicians over God.

2 Ne 4:34

"Cursed is he that putteth his trust in the arm of flesh."

Teaches: Nephi's warning names Asa's exact drift. The question isn't whether we use human help — it's what we rest our weight on.
Jehoshaphat · 2 Chr 18

Micaiah stands alone against 400 court prophets: "what my God saith, that will I speak" — and lands in prison.

Mosiah 12–17

Abinadi testifies alone before King Noah's priests, sealing his witness with his life — and converting Alma.

Teaches: One voice with God's word outweighs any majority — and you never know who's listening (Micaiah's word felled a king; Abinadi's founded a church).
Uzziah · 2 Chr 26

A king seizes the priest's office as if success entitled him to it.

Mosiah 11:5–6

King Noah treats priesthood as a royal possession, installing proud flatterers in place of consecrated priests.

Teaches: Authority in God's house is given by calling, never taken by ambition — "no man taketh this honour unto himself" (Hebrews 5:4).
Hezekiah · 2 Chr 30

Letters of welcome go to a scattered, wounded people; the unready are pardoned and healed at the feast.

Alma 5:33–34

"He sendeth an invitation unto all men, for the arms of mercy are extended towards them."

Teaches: God's table is set by invitation, not audition. Mercy holds the door while hearts prepare — then, as in both stories, He heals.
Jehoshaphat · 2 Chr 20:20

"Believe in the Lord your God, so shall ye be established; believe his prophets, so shall ye prosper."

1 Ne 15:24–25

Those who hearken to and hold fast the word of God never perish — the iron rod through the mist.

Teaches: Every deliverance this week began with heeding a prophet — Azariah, Micaiah, Jahaziel, Azariah the priest, Isaiah. That's still the pattern.

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Pattern hunt: split the class into four groups (Asa, Jehoshaphat, Uzziah, Hezekiah), each with a one-paragraph summary and key verses. Have each report "where did he put his trust, and what happened?" — then let them discover they've all told the same story. Cap it with Helaman 12:1–2.
  • Four-kings scoreboard: draw a chart on the board — each king rated on "sought the Lord" at the start, middle, and end of his reign. Ask which king surprised them most, and why endings matter more than beginnings.
  • Object lesson for the title verse: have a youth walk a straight line on the floor while staring at a fixed point across the room, then again while looking at their feet. "Our eyes are upon thee" (20:12) is a navigation strategy, not just a prayer.
  • Doctrinal-diamond memory cards: put the week's five diamonds (14:11; 15:7; 18:13; 20:15; 26:5) on cards; pairs draw one, read it in context, and teach the class in 60 seconds why it's "of great price."
  • Closer — match each king to Christ: list the four kings on one side and four attributes of the Savior on the other (the Physician who gives rest · the One who fights our battle · the meek High Priest · the Lamb who casts out none) and let the class connect them. End on the pull quote below.

Tie to Jesus Christ

Four kings, one King: each of this week's rulers — at his best or by his failure — points to Jesus Christ.

Asa → the Physician and the Rest

Asa's finest hour was resting his whole weight on God (14:11); his tragedy was dying without seeking the Healer (16:12). Jesus is both things Asa needed: "Come unto me … and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28), and the Physician who came for the sick (Mark 2:17). And Hanani's promise — the Lord's eyes running "to and fro throughout the whole earth" looking for hearts to strengthen (16:9) — is Christ's seeking, shepherding character (Luke 15:4).

Jehoshaphat → eyes on Him, because the battle is His

"Our eyes are upon thee" (20:12) is the posture of faith in Christ: "look to God and live" (Alma 37:47; John 3:14–15). And "the battle is not yours, but God's" (20:15) is a type of the Atonement — the decisive fight against sin and death was one we had no might to win, and He fought it alone (D&C 76:107). Even the choir marching ahead of the army foreshadows Him: Jesus sang a hymn on His way to Gethsemane (Matthew 26:30).

Uzziah → the meek King the proud king wasn't

Uzziah grasped at the priest's office; Christ, the true High Priest, "glorified not himself" (Hebrews 5:4–5) and described His own heart as "meek and lowly" (Matthew 11:29). Where Uzziah's pride made him a leper cut off from the temple, Jesus touched lepers and made them clean (Mark 1:41–42). And the year Uzziah died, Isaiah saw the King whose throne never falls — "I saw also the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up" (Isaiah 6:1): Jehovah, the premortal Christ.

Hezekiah → the Passover Lamb who casts out none

Every lamb at Hezekiah's feast prophesied of Jesus: "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" (1 Corinthians 5:7). The king's letter — God "will not turn away his face from you, if ye return" (30:9) — and his prayer for unready guests are the Savior's own promise in advance: "him that cometh to me I will in no wise cast out" (John 6:37). The Prince of Peace still blesses peacemakers (Matthew 5:9; 3 Nephi 12:9) — and still heals every one that prepareth his heart.
