Historical Summary of the Period

A leap forward in time. This week's reading jumps about a century past Jehoshaphat. Uzziah (also called Azariah in 2 Kings) reigned roughly 792–740 BC — a 52-year reign, the second-longest in Judah's history, including years as co-regent with his father Amaziah and later years when his son Jotham governed for him. He came to the throne at sixteen (26:1, 3).

Judah's golden age. Uzziah's reign was a burst of national success: he defeated the Philistines and Arabians, received tribute from Ammon, rebuilt the port of Eloth on the Red Sea, raised towers in Jerusalem and the desert, dug many wells, and — a charming detail — "he loved husbandry" (26:10): the king was a farmer at heart, with vineyards in the hills and herds in the lowlands. His engineers built "engines, invented by cunning men" for shooting arrows and hurling stones from the towers (26:15) — some of the earliest mechanical artillery mentioned in scripture. His fame "spread far abroad … even to the entering in of Egypt."

The prophets who knew him. Uzziah's reign frames some of scripture's greatest voices. Amos dates his prophecy "two years before the earthquake" in Uzziah's days (Amos 1:1) — a quake so severe that Zechariah still used it as a byword 250 years later (Zechariah 14:5), and archaeologists have found mid-8th-century destruction layers at sites like Hazor consistent with a massive quake. And Isaiah anchors his temple vision to this king: "In the year that king Uzziah died I saw also the Lord" (Isaiah 6:1) — about 740 BC.

The tablet with his name on it. In 1931, Professor E. L. Sukenik identified a stone plaque from a Mount of Olives convent collection — the Uzziah Tablet, now in the Israel Museum. Its Aramaic inscription, written centuries later when his bones were relocated, reads to the effect of: here were brought the bones of Uzziah, king of Judah — do not open. Even his burial matches the chronicle: because of his leprosy he was buried "in the field of the burial which belonged to the kings" but not in the royal tombs themselves (26:23), and later generations had to move and re-mark his remains.

Culture note: why the incense mattered

Burning incense on the golden altar was reserved by the law of Moses to consecrated priests, the sons of Aaron (Exodus 30:7–8; Numbers 16:40). Israel had already learned this at terrible cost — King Saul lost his dynasty over an unauthorized sacrifice, and Korah's rebellion ended in disaster over this exact issue. Uzziah knew all of this. His trespass wasn't ignorance; it was entitlement.

Chapter Summary

1
26:1–5

A teachable king

Sixteen-year-old Uzziah "sought God in the days of Zechariah, who had understanding in the visions of God" — he had a spiritual mentor, and he listened. The verse the manual flags as a doctrinal diamond states the whole chapter's law: "as long as he sought the Lord, God made him to prosper" (26:5). Note the words as long as.

2
26:6–15

Marvellously helped

Victories, tribute, towers, wells, vineyards, a 307,500-man army, engineered war machines. The chronicler is careful about the credit: "he was marvellously helped, till he was strong." Every line of the resume was a gift.

3
26:16

The turning point — one verse

"But when he was strong, his heart was lifted up to his destruction." Strength itself became the trap. He entered the temple to burn incense on the altar — seizing for himself an ordinance God had given only to consecrated priests.

4
26:17–20

Eighty men of valour say no

Azariah the priest and eighty "valiant men" follow the king in and withstand him: "It appertaineth not unto thee, Uzziah." Confronting an armed, famous, furious king took real courage. Uzziah rages, censer in hand — and leprosy rises in his forehead. The priests thrust him out; "himself hasted also to go out, because the Lord had smitten him."

5
26:21–23

The long quiet ending

Uzziah lives out his days a leper in a separate house, "cut off from the house of the Lord" — the very place he had barged into. Jotham his son governs. At death he is buried near, but not with, the kings: "for they said, He is a leper." Isaiah, who chronicled his reign (26:22), receives his great vision of the true King the year Uzziah dies.

A hard question, answered honestly

Why leprosy — isn't that harsh? Under the law of Moses, leprosy rendered a person ritually unclean and barred from the temple (Leviticus 13). The consequence fit the trespass with terrible precision: the man who claimed more access to the temple than God had given him received less — permanently. It's not random cruelty; it's a sobering picture of what pride actually does: it doesn't promote us, it cuts us off. And note God's mercy in the timing — the warning came first, through Azariah. Uzziah chose rage over retreat.

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

Mormon watched Uzziah's pattern destroy an entire civilization — and named it for us.

2 Chr 26:5

"As long as he sought the Lord, God made him to prosper" — prosperity on a condition.

2 Ne 1:20

"Inasmuch as ye shall keep my commandments ye shall prosper in the land" — the covenant promise of the Book of Mormon, repeated in nearly every generation.

Teaches: Both hemispheres lived under the same two-way promise. The condition never expires — for kings or for us.
2 Chr 26:15–16

Helped → strong → heart lifted up → destruction. Uzziah's arc in four beats.

Helaman 12:1–2

Mormon's lament: at "the very time" the Lord prospers His people, "then is the time that they do harden their hearts, and do forget the Lord."

Teaches: The pride cycle isn't a Nephite quirk — it's human gravity. Uzziah ran the whole cycle in a single lifetime.
2 Chr 26:16–18

Uzziah seizes a priestly ordinance that "appertaineth not" to him.

Mosiah 11:5–6

King Noah dismisses his father's consecrated priests and installs proud flatterers — royal power treating priesthood as a possession.

Teaches: Priesthood authority is God's to give, never ours to take — "no man taketh this honour unto himself, but he that is called of God" (Hebrews 5:4).
2 Chr 26:17–18

Azariah and eighty priests confront the most powerful man in the kingdom to his face.

Helaman 8–9

Nephi, alone on his garden tower, rebukes corrupt judges and an angry crowd — and God vindicates his words.

Teaches: God's servants must sometimes tell power "no." Courage in His errand is always backed up.

Tie to Come, Follow Me

Within this week's outline — "Our Eyes Are upon Thee" (July 20–26) — Uzziah is the cautionary chapter: what happens when the eyes come off the Lord and settle on the mirror.

What the manual points to in this chapter

  • "Pride leads to destruction" (2 Chronicles 26:3–21). The outline notes that, like many kings of Judah, Uzziah's reign "began with great accomplishments but ended with tragedy," and asks you to find the pattern — and to name the turning point in his life.
  • The incense context. The manual supplies the key background for vv. 16–23: only priests were allowed to burn incense in the temple. Its discussion questions: why do you think Uzziah disobeyed God? What do you learn from this tragic experience?
  • Doctrinal diamond: the outline's diamond list includes 26:5 — "as long as he sought the Lord, God made him to prosper."
  • Music tie: the outline points to the hymn "Be Thou Humble" (Hymns, no. 130), which promises that the Lord will lead the humble by the hand and give them answers to their prayers.
  • For children: "I can be humble" — build a tower of blocks or cups, naming one of Uzziah's accomplishments (26:3–15) per piece; read v. 16 and discuss hearts "lifted up"; then let them knock the tower down and talk about staying humble.

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Upgrade the children's tower activity for teens: stack cups while listing Uzziah's wins from vv. 6–15 (have them find each in the text), read v. 16, then topple it. Ask: which cup was the first mistake — or was it none of the cups and something invisible?
  • Turning-point vote: give the class two minutes with chapter 26 and have everyone vote on the single verse where it all changed. (Most will land on v. 16 — then discuss the manual's question of why success made him feel the rules no longer applied.)
  • Modern mirrors: where do we see "marvellously helped, till he was strong" today — athletes, influencers, even ourselves after a success streak? What keeps blessings from curdling into entitlement?
  • Spotlight Azariah's eighty: it took courage to tell a king no. Discuss a time it took courage to (kindly) tell a friend they were out of line — and what made it loving rather than judgmental.
  • Sing or read "Be Thou Humble" (Hymns 130) and list the two promises God makes to the humble in its verses. Contrast each promise with what Uzziah got instead.

Tie to Jesus Christ

Uzziah points to Christ by contrast — everywhere the proud king grasped, the true King gave.

The King who did not seize the honor

Uzziah, a king, seized the priest's office. Jesus — actually entitled to every office — took the opposite path: "Christ glorified not himself to be made an high priest," but was called of God (Hebrews 5:4–5). The King of kings knelt with a towel and washed feet (John 13). Uzziah teaches what Christ embodies: real greatness stoops.

"Learn of me; for I am meek and lowly"

The chapter's principle — pride leads to destruction — is the negative image of the Savior's self-description: "learn of me; for I am meek and lowly in heart: and ye shall find rest unto your souls" (Matthew 11:29). Uzziah's strength cost him his rest; Christ's meekness is where rest is found. That's the trade the lesson invites every student to make.

The Healer of what pride causes

Uzziah's pride left him a leper — cut off from the temple and from his people. When Jesus met lepers, He did what no one else would: "Jesus, moved with compassion, put forth his hand, and touched him … and immediately the leprosy departed" (Mark 1:41–42). Sin separates; Christ crosses the separation and heals it. No one is too cut off for Him — not even someone whose wound was self-inflicted.

When the earthly king fell, Isaiah saw the true King

"In the year that king Uzziah died I saw also the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up" (Isaiah 6:1). The Lord Isaiah saw — Jehovah — is the premortal Jesus Christ. The timing is the sermon: the year Judah's proud, fallen king was buried outside the royal tombs, heaven showed a prophet the King whose throne never falls. Notice the wordplay: Uzziah's heart was "lifted up" to destruction; the Lord alone is rightly "high and lifted up."
