Historical Summary of the Period

When and where. Asa reigned about 911–870 BC, roughly twenty years after Solomon's united kingdom split in two (c. 931 BC). He was the third king of the southern Kingdom of Judah — the small nation centered on Jerusalem and its temple — while the larger northern Kingdom of Israel sat on his border, often hostile. His reign lasted an unusually long 41 years (2 Chronicles 16:13).

The religious mess he inherited. Judah was littered with pagan altars, sacred pillars, and groves for idol worship left over from Solomon's later years and the reigns of Rehoboam and Abijah. Asa's first act was demolition: he tore down the foreign altars and "high places," smashed the images, and commanded Judah to seek the Lord (14:2–5). He even deposed his own grandmother Maachah from her royal position because she had made an idol (15:16) — reform that cost him family comfort.

The Zerah invasion. Around 900 BC, "Zerah the Ethiopian" (Hebrew Cushite, from the region south of Egypt) marched north with an enormous force — the text says "a thousand thousand," an idiom for an uncountable host — and met Judah at Mareshah in the Shephelah, the low foothills southwest of Jerusalem. Mareshah is a real, excavated site (Tel Maresha, in today's Bet Guvrin national park), one of the cities 2 Chronicles 11 says Rehoboam had fortified. Judah's smaller army won a rout the chronicler credits entirely to God.

The Baasha crisis and the Syrian treaty. Late in Asa's reign, King Baasha of northern Israel fortified Ramah — a chokepoint only about five miles north of Jerusalem — essentially blockading Judah's capital. Instead of praying as he had at Mareshah, Asa emptied the temple treasury and bought an alliance with Ben-hadad of Syria (Aram-Damascus), paying a pagan king to attack Israel from behind. It worked militarily — and failed spiritually. The seer Hanani rebuked him, and Asa threw him in prison: the first recorded imprisonment of a prophet in Judah's history.

Why Chronicles tells it this way

Chronicles was compiled after the Babylonian exile (c. 450–400 BC) for Jews rebuilding their nation, and it retells Judah's history as a series of test cases in one question: what happens when a king relies on the Lord — and when he doesn't? That's why Asa gets three full chapters here but a brief notice in 1 Kings 15. See the Bible Dictionary entry "Chronicles."

Chapter Summary

1
14:1–8

Reform brings rest

Asa "did that which was good and right," removed idolatry, and commanded Judah to seek the Lord. The result is stated twice: "the land had rest" and "the Lord had given him rest." He uses the quiet years wisely — building fortified cities while there is peace.

2
14:9–15

The impossible battle at Mareshah

Zerah's overwhelming Cushite army arrives. Asa's battle plan is a prayer: "it is nothing with thee to help, whether with many, or with them that have no power: help us, O Lord our God; for we rest on thee" (14:11). The Lord smites the Ethiopians and Judah carries away the spoil.

3
15:1–7

Azariah's prophecy

The Spirit comes on the prophet Azariah, who meets the returning army with the thesis of the whole lesson: "The Lord is with you, while ye be with him; and if ye seek him, he will be found of you; but if ye forsake him, he will forsake you." He adds: "be ye strong … for your work shall be rewarded" (15:7).

4
15:8–19

Covenant renewed with joy

Encouraged, Asa deepens the reform. The people — including refugees from northern Israel who defect "when they saw that the Lord his God was with him" — gather in Jerusalem and covenant to seek the Lord "with all their heart." They swear it with shouting and trumpets, "and all Judah rejoiced at the oath." Even the queen mother loses her position over her idol.

5
16:1–10

The turning: silver instead of prayer

Thirty-five years in, Baasha blockades Judah at Ramah. Asa — the man who once prayed down a million-man army — now raids the temple treasury to hire Ben-hadad of Syria. The seer Hanani confronts him: you were delivered from a vast army "because thou didst rely on the Lord"; now "thou hast done foolishly." Asa rages and imprisons the prophet, and oppresses some of the people.

6
16:11–14

The sad finish

In his 39th year Asa's feet become severely diseased, "yet in his disease he sought not to the Lord, but to the physicians." He dies two years later. The tragedy isn't that he saw doctors — it's that he had stopped seeking God at all.

A hard question, answered honestly

Was it wrong for Asa to see physicians? No — the rebuke is about the pattern, not the medicine. In chapter 14 Asa's first move in crisis was God; in chapter 16 his first, second, and only moves are human ones: Syrian soldiers for a war, physicians for a disease, prison for a prophet. Hanani's key verse explains what Asa forfeited: "the eyes of the Lord run to and fro throughout the whole earth, to shew himself strong in the behalf of them whose heart is perfect toward him" (16:9). God was still watching for a reason to help him. Asa stopped giving Him one.

Comparison to the Book of Mormon

The Book of Mormon tells Asa's story again and again — sometimes as promise, sometimes as warning.

2 Chronicles 14:11

Facing Zerah's uncountable host, Asa's whole strategy is a battlefield prayer: "we rest on thee."

Alma 2:27–31

Facing a combined Amlicite-Lamanite army "as numerous almost … as the sands of the sea," Alma cries to God in the middle of combat and is "strengthened."

Teaches: Prayer isn't preparation for the battle — it can be the battle plan itself.
2 Chronicles 15:2

"The Lord is with you, while ye be with him; and if ye seek him, he will be found of you."

Alma 37:13

"If ye will keep my commandments ye shall prosper in the land" — the same conditional promise, taught to Helaman as the key to the whole Nephite record.

Teaches: God's presence follows a covenant condition — and He states the terms plainly, in both hemispheres.
2 Chronicles 15:12–15

Judah covenants "with all their heart," swears with shouting and trumpets, "and all Judah rejoiced at the oath … and the Lord gave them rest."

Mosiah 5:2, 5

King Benjamin's people, hearts changed, covenant "to do his will … all the remainder of our days" — and receive joy and a new name.

Teaches: Covenant-making is meant to be a whole-heart, whole-community event — and it produces joy, not burden.
2 Chronicles 16:1–10

Asa buys Syrian help with temple treasure, then imprisons the seer who rebukes him.

2 Nephi 4:34

"Cursed is he that putteth his trust in the arm of flesh" — Nephi's warning names Asa's exact mistake, trusting borrowed human strength over God.

Teaches: Strength borrowed from the world always costs more than it saves — Asa paid with the temple's treasure and his own peace.

Tie to Come, Follow Me

This week's outline — "Our Eyes Are upon Thee" (July 20–26) — opens its study ideas with Asa, under the principle heading below.

What the manual points to in these chapters

  • "Trusting in God will bring me peace" (2 Chronicles 14–16). The manual gives a three-row study table — 14:9–12, 15:1–8, and 16:1–10 — asking two questions of each passage: what challenge did Asa face, and where did he put his trust? Then it asks: "Why do we sometimes stop trusting the Lord?"
  • Doctrinal diamonds. Quoting Elder Neal A. Maxwell on the scriptures' "doctrinal diamonds," the manual invites you to mark short, powerful statements — and its first two examples are in these chapters: 14:11 and 15:7.
  • Conference connection. The outline points to Elder D. Todd Christofferson, "Our Relationship with God" (Liahona, May 2022) — on how our trust in God grows out of a real relationship with Him.
  • For children, the outline pairs Asa with Jehoshaphat under "I can trust God," suggesting an umbrella object lesson: we trust an umbrella to keep us dry — why do we trust the Lord to keep us spiritually safe? (See 14:11.)

Teaching ideas for youth (ages 12–13)

  • Fill out the manual's trust table together (14:9–12; 15:1–8; 16:1–10), then have the class rate Asa's trust in God from 1–10 at each stage. Watch them discover the decline themselves.
  • Object lesson: two chairs, one sturdy and one wobbly. Which one do you "rest" your full weight on? Asa's word in 14:11 is rest — where he rested changed between chapter 14 and chapter 16.
  • Doctrinal-diamond hunt: give two minutes to mark 14:11 or 15:7, then have each youth rewrite their verse as a text message to a friend having a hard week.
  • Discuss the manual's question straight-up: why do we sometimes stop trusting the Lord? List modern "Ben-hadads" — things teens hire to solve problems before they've prayed about them.
  • Finish-strong discussion: in a race, a fast start matters less than the finish. Asa was faithful for 35 years and drifted in five. What daily habits keep trust alive for a whole lifetime?

Tie to Jesus Christ

Asa's best moments echo the Savior's invitations — and his worst moment shows exactly who he stopped turning to.

The rest Asa found is a Person

Twice chapter 14 says the Lord gave Asa "rest" (14:6–7), and Asa's great prayer says "we rest on thee" (14:11). Centuries later, Jesus claimed that role in His own voice: "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28). The rest Judah felt between wars is a small taste of the rest Christ offers permanently.

"While ye be with him" — Christ said it too

Azariah's promise, "The Lord is with you, while ye be with him" (15:2), is the Old Testament form of the Savior's teaching in the vineyard: "Abide in me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself, except it abide in the vine; no more can ye, except ye abide in me" (John 15:4). Both teach that closeness to God is a living connection we maintain — not a one-time achievement.

The Physician Asa never called

Diseased and dying, Asa "sought not to the Lord, but to the physicians" (16:12). Jesus called Himself the physician Asa needed: "They that are whole have no need of the physician, but they that are sick: I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance" (Mark 2:17). Christ heals bodies, but His specialty is the disease Asa actually had — a hardened heart. Medicine and faith aren't rivals; the tragedy is a soul that no longer seeks the Healer at all.

His eyes run to and fro — seeking us

Hanani's verse — "the eyes of the Lord run to and fro throughout the whole earth, to shew himself strong" for the loyal-hearted (16:9) — reveals Christ's character: He is actively searching for people to help, like the shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine to find the one (Luke 15:4). This week's theme, "our eyes are upon thee," has an answer: His eyes are already upon us.
